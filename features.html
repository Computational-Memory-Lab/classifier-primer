Chapter 3

From EEG to 280 numbers

A classifier cannot read a squiggle. It reads a list of numbers. This chapter is about how we turn one into the other — and why that step is where most of the interesting decisions get made.

What one trial actually is

When a word appears on screen, we save a slice of EEG around it. In this study that slice runs from 100 ms before the word to 1,200 ms after it, and every electrode is sampled 250 times a second — so the slice is 325 measurements per electrode, 1.3 seconds in total.

Those two numbers are choices, and both have a reason. The 100 ms before the word is the baseline: nothing has happened yet, so whatever the voltage is doing there is drift and background noise rather than a response. Subtracting its average from the rest of the trial puts every trial on a common zero, which is what makes trials comparable at all. The 1,200 ms after is long enough to contain the responses anyone expects to matter — the visual response by ~200 ms, the memory-related effects between 300 and 800 ms — with room to spare. Stop at 500 ms and you would cut off the later effects; run to 3 seconds and you would mostly be recording the next trial.

The shape of one participant's data
That is 37.6 million numbers per participant, about 140 MB on disk. Multiply by 67 people and you have the 8.7 GB sitting on the lab Drive.

We cannot hand a classifier 83,200 numbers per trial, and the reason is worth understanding now rather than later.

A classifier learns one weight per input. With 83,200 inputs and only 450 trials to learn from, it has far more knobs to turn than facts to constrain them — so there will always be some combination of weights that separates these 450 trials perfectly, even when the inputs are pure noise. The model can simply assign weights that memorise which trial is which, the way you could draw a line through any two points. It has learned the trials rather than the pattern, so it scores beautifully on them and no better than chance on anything new. That is overfitting, and it is the central hazard of this whole enterprise; chapter 4 lets you watch it happen.

The defence is to give the model far fewer inputs than you have trials. So we reduce, and two decisions do almost all the reducing: which electrodes, and how finely in time.

Decision 1: ten electrodes out of 256

The pipeline keeps ten. In the code they are a bare list of numbers with no names attached, which makes them look arbitrary:

electrodes = [64 194 21 41 214 8 101 87 153 137]

They are not. Plot them on the scalp and the design is obvious: four sit on the midline, front to back, and the other six are three left/right mirror pairs. Between them they sample the whole head — front, centre, back, left and right — with as few electrodes as that coverage allows.

Why ten, and why these ten?

Why so few: the overfitting argument above. Ten electrodes × 28 bins is 280 inputs against 450 trials, which is already a stretch; all 256 electrodes would be 7,168 inputs and hopeless.

Why these: even coverage of the scalp, following the earlier study this project builds on (Chakravarty et al., 2020) so the results can be compared with it. The map shows the logic — a midline chain plus symmetric pairs. Two of them are the sites the classic ERP analysis uses anyway: Fz for the FN400 and P3 for the LPP.

What is not known: whether a different ten would do better. Nobody has swept it. It is on the list of unclaimed threads at the end of chapter 7.

Decision 2: chop time into overlapping bins

325 time points per electrode is still too many, and neighbouring points are nearly identical anyway. So we slide a 100 ms window along the trace in 40 ms steps, and average the voltage inside each window. Each window becomes one number.

Because the step is smaller than the window, consecutive bins overlap by 60 ms. That is deliberate: it means a feature is never split awkwardly across a boundary.

Watch the binning happen
Drag to move the window along the real waveform. The bar underneath is the feature value that window produces.

The window starts at 0 ms — the baseline before the word is skipped entirely, since it is by definition the period before anything happened. Sliding to the end gives 28 bins, the last one finishing at about 1,176 ms.

10 electrodes × 28 bins = 280 features. That is the number you will see everywhere in this project. One trial, which started as 83,200 numbers, is now a list of 280.

The finished product

Here is what the classifier actually gets: a table with one row per trial and 280 columns, plus a single answer column saying what happened on that trial. This is real — it is participant 45's feature matrix, computed from their EEG with the same recipe the MATLAB pipeline uses.

Participant 45's feature matrix, X
450 trials × 280 features. Colour is voltage: blue is negative, red positive. Hover any cell. Rows are sorted by outcome so the four conditions are grouped.

Look at it honestly: it looks like static. There is no visible band that separates Hits from Misses, no obvious stripe where memory lives. If the effect were plain to the eye we would not need a classifier at all.

That is the situation in a sentence. The information is in there — chapter 5 will pull it out, above chance, on this exact matrix — but it is spread thinly across many features, and no single one carries much of it. Finding structure in a table that looks like this is precisely what classifiers are for.

Everything else on that list is a choice too

The 100 ms window, the 40 ms step, the ten electrodes, using raw voltage rather than something derived from it — none of these were handed down. Somebody picked each one, and a different choice would give a different answer.

That is not a flaw in this project. It is the project. The whole point of the manuscript is to work out which of these choices actually matter, so future studies can stop guessing. Chapter 6 shows what happens when you vary them.
