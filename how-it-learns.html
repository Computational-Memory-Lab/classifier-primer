Chapter 4

How a classifier actually learns

No equations. We will use two features instead of 280 so you can see everything, and build up to the real thing.

Drawing a line

Imagine each trial is a dot. Its position is set by two measurements from the EEG. Blue dots were Hits, green dots were Misses. The classifier's job is to find a rule that separates them.

For the classifiers this lab uses, that rule is a straight line — everything on one side gets called “Hit”, everything on the other “Miss”. Drag the line. Grab the middle to move it, or either end to rotate it, and watch the mistakes update.

Find the best line you can
Drag the black line. The tallies underneath are live.

Two things worth noticing. First, when the clouds overlap you cannot get everything right — no line exists that does. Every position you choose trades one kind of mistake against the other. Second, “Let the classifier solve it” does not produce a magic line. It produces the line that a specific rule — linear discriminant analysis, the workhorse of this project — says is best given where the clouds sit.

Real EEG lives at the left-hand end of that slider. Drag separability down towards 15 or 20 and look at the overlap. That is what a real Hit/Miss problem looks like, and it is why the AUCs in chapter 6 are 0.58 rather than 0.95.

From a line to a score

Forcing a yes/no answer throws information away. A trial right next to the line is a coin flip; one far from it is a confident call. So instead of a decision, the classifier gives each trial a score — how far it is from the line, and which side.

Collapse the dots onto a single axis running perpendicular to the line and you get two distributions. Now the threshold is a slider you can move.

One threshold, three views
Move the threshold. Watch the mistakes trade off, and watch the point ride along the ROC curve.

So what is the ROC curve?

Every possible threshold gives one pair of numbers: the fraction of Hits you caught, and the fraction of Misses you wrongly called Hits. Plot that pair for every threshold and you trace out the curve. A useless classifier gives the diagonal — it catches Hits and raises false alarms at exactly the same rate. A good one bulges towards the top-left, where you catch a lot and mistake few.

ROC is the receiver operating characteristic — an odd name with a literal history. It was developed for radar operators in the 1940s, who had to decide how faint a blip on a screen counted as an aircraft. Set the threshold low and you catch every aircraft but scramble fighters at flocks of birds; set it high and you miss real ones. The curve described how a given receiver traded those two errors off as its threshold was operated — hence the name. The problem is identical here, with Hits for aircraft and Misses for birds, which is why psychology borrowed the tool wholesale.
AUC is the area under that curve — and it equals the probability that a randomly chosen Hit scores higher than a randomly chosen Miss. The diagonal cuts the square in half, so a useless classifier scores 0.5. Filling the whole square means every Hit outranks every Miss, so a perfect one scores 1.0. Because it sweeps every threshold, you never have to pick one, and because it only cares about ordering, it is immune to lopsided classes.

Three classifiers, three ideas

“Let the classifier solve it” produced a line. But which line is best is a matter of opinion, and different classifiers hold different opinions. This project uses three, and their names all follow the same pattern — a word describing the idea, then what it operates on.

The same trials, two opinions about where the line goes
LDA and SVM, on identical data. Switch between them and watch which trials each one is actually paying attention to. (The RVM is described below but not drawn: what makes it different is not visible in two dimensions.)

LDA — Linear Discriminant Analysis

A discriminant is a rule that discriminates: feed it a trial, it returns a number, and the sign of that number is your answer. Linear means the rule is a weighted sum of the features and nothing fancier — no squaring, no interactions, just w₁×feature₁ + w₂×feature₂ + …, which geometrically is a straight line (or in 280 dimensions, a flat plane).

LDA's opinion is that each class is a cloud, and the line belongs where the two clouds are equally plausible. It measures where each cloud's centre is, and how the cloud is stretched and tilted, and then draws the boundary accordingly. Crucially it uses every trial to do this: a trial far from the boundary still helps pin down where its cloud sits and how wide it is.

That is its strength here. It answers in one step of arithmetic, with nothing to tune and nothing to converge, and its assumption — two clouds of roughly the same shape — is a strong one. Strong assumptions are exactly what you want when you have 450 trials and 280 features, because they leave the model less room to invent structure that is not there. Chapter 6 shows LDA quietly beating the more sophisticated option.

SVM — Support Vector Machine

The SVM's opinion is the opposite: the clouds are irrelevant, only the borderline trials matter. It looks for the line with the widest margin — the biggest gap between the line and the nearest trial on either side. Push the line anywhere else and some trial gets closer to it, so the margin shrinks.

The trials that end up touching that margin are the support vectors — they support the boundary, in the sense that they alone hold it in place. This is the striking part: move a trial that is far from the line and the answer does not change at all. Only the borderline cases have a vote. A “machine” here is just old terminology for a learning procedure.

Because it ignores everything except the boundary cases, an SVM is unbothered by oddly-shaped clouds and outliers far from the line. The price is that it throws away the information LDA was using, and when classes overlap as heavily as ours do, almost every trial ends up near the boundary anyway. One knob controls how much it tolerates trials on the wrong side of the margin — a tighter setting fits the training trials harder, which is the hazard the next section is about.

RVM — Relevance Vector Machine

The RVM (Tipping, 2001) is the SVM's question asked in a Bayesian way. It makes predictions from the same kind of ingredients, but instead of solving for the widest margin, it starts by allowing every trial to contribute and then lets the arithmetic switch off the ones it cannot justify keeping. The survivors are the relevance vectors — the trials the model decided were genuinely relevant.

Two things follow. It usually keeps far fewer trials than an SVM keeps support vectors, so the final rule is simpler. And because it is Bayesian it returns a probability rather than a bare distance from a line — it can say “70% likely a Hit” rather than just “Hit”.

It also has the most ways to go wrong. It has to be told how to compare two trials (its kernel), and in 280 dimensions the intuitive choices misbehave badly: every pair of trials is roughly equidistant, so a distance-based comparison finds every trial equally unlike every other, keeps nothing, and predicts the same value for everything. AUC then comes out at exactly 0.500 — not because there is no signal, but because the model collapsed.

An AUC of exactly 0.500 is a symptom, not a result. Real chance performance wobbles around 0.5 — it lands on 0.497, 0.512, 0.503. A clean 0.500 to three decimals usually means the model gave every trial an identical score, so every pair tied. Something collapsed. It is worth knowing the difference before you report one.

The trap: learning the noise

Here is the thing that makes machine learning hard, and the reason for all the care that follows.

A model with enough freedom can fit anything, including the random wobble in your particular sample. It will look brilliant on the trials it learned from, and fall apart on new ones. That is overfitting, and it is not a hypothetical — with 280 features and sometimes fewer than 100 trials per class, this project is squarely in the danger zone.

“Flexibility” is not a vague quality — it is a count. It is the number of knobs the model is allowed to turn, and every knob is one more thing it can fit to your particular sample. In the demo below the model is a curve, and flexibility is how many bends the curve may have: at 1 it can only be a straight line, at 3 it can bend twice, at 14 it can wander through almost any set of points you like.

The classifiers in this project have the same knob, wearing a different name. Each of the 280 features gets its own weight, so a classifier given all 280 has 280 knobs; give it one electrode's 28 bins and it has 28. That is why chapter 3 spent so long cutting 83,200 numbers down to 280 — and why chapter 5's regularization slider, which limits how extreme those weights may get, is doing the same job as this one.

The same model, scored two ways
Flexibility = how many bends the curve is allowed. Turn it up and watch the two lines go opposite ways — that gap is overfitting.

The dashed line is what the model scores on the trials it trained on. It only ever goes up — of course it does; you are marking your own homework. The solid line is what it scores on trials held back. That one turns around and heads down, because past a point the model is memorizing noise that will not repeat.

This is why a suspiciously high AUC is bad news, not good news. If someone reports 0.9 on single-trial memory EEG, the first hypothesis is that test data leaked into training somehow. In this field, big numbers are a symptom.

The fix: cross-validation

We know the rule: never judge the model on trials it trained on. The obvious way to obey it is to split the data once — train on 80% of the trials, test on the remaining 20%, report that. This is called a holdout, and for a study like this it is a bad deal for two reasons.

It wastes data. Those 90 test trials taught the model nothing, and 90 out of 450 is a lot to spend when data is this expensive — every trial is a person sitting in a lab wearing 256 electrodes.

And the answer depends on which 20% you happened to pick. Test on 90 trials and your AUC is estimated from 90 trials, which is a small and noisy sample. Draw a different 20% and you get a visibly different number, with no way to tell which is closer to the truth.

Cross-validation gets around both. The idea: do the holdout repeatedly, so that every trial takes a turn being held out — and average.

What a fold is

A fold is just one of the equal piles you deal the trials into. Ten folds of 450 trials means ten piles of 45. Nothing more exotic than that; the word is dealing terminology, not mathematics.

The piles are stratified, which means each one gets the same mix of classes as the whole set. If 33% of the trials are Misses, each fold gets roughly 33% Misses. Deal them at random and one fold might, by chance, contain almost no Misses at all — and a fold with only one class in it cannot be scored, because AUC needs both.

The rotation

Now run the holdout ten times, holding out a different pile each round:

  • Round 1 — train on folds 2–10 (405 trials), test on fold 1 (45 trials).
  • Round 2 — train on folds 1 and 3–10, test on fold 2.
  • …and so on, until every fold has been the test set exactly once.

Each round trains a fresh model from scratch — nothing carries over — and scores it only on the pile that model never saw. So the rule is never broken. But because the rounds between them cover all ten piles, every one of the 450 trials has been predicted exactly once by a model that had not seen it. No trial is wasted, and none is graded by a model that knew the answer.

Averaging the ten scores gives the participant's AUC. That average is a much steadier estimate than any single holdout, because it rests on all 450 trials rather than a lucky or unlucky 90.

Why ten, and why five for false alarms

The number of folds is a trade. More folds means more training data per round (with ten, each model sees 90% of the trials) and therefore a model closer to the one you would get from everything — but it also means each test pile is smaller and noisier, and it means training the model ten times over. Ten is the conventional compromise, and it is what this pipeline uses for Old/New and Hit vs Miss.

FA vs CR gets five folds instead, and the reason is participant 45's 14 false alarms. Split 14 across ten folds and each holds one or two; a single unlucky deal leaves a fold with none, that fold cannot be scored, and the ones that can are scored on a single false alarm apiece. Five folds means about three false alarms per fold — still thin, but workable. When the rare class is that rare, the fold count stops being a free choice.

All of this happens within one participant. We never train on Alice and test on Bob. Head shape, electrode placement and the anatomy underneath all differ enough that a weighting learned on one person means something different on the next, so each person gets their own classifier and their own AUC. Only then do we ask whether those AUCs beat chance across the group — which is chapter 6.

One more wrinkle: lopsided classes

Participant 45 gave 14 false alarms and 211 correct rejections. A classifier trained on that will notice the cheapest possible strategy — always say “correct rejection” — and take it.

The pipeline's answer is SMOTE — Synthetic Minority Over-sampling Technique (Chawla et al., 2002). The name says what it does: it over-samples the minority class by synthesising new members of it.

The obvious way to balance classes is to duplicate the rare ones — copy the 14 false alarms fifteen times each. That fails, because exact copies tell the model nothing new; it just weights those same 14 points more heavily and memorizes them harder. SMOTE's insight is to make new points that are plausible rather than identical:

  1. Pick one of the 14 real false alarms at random.
  2. Find its nearest neighbours among the other false alarms — the trials most like it. The pipeline uses the closest four.
  3. Pick one of those neighbours, and place a new point somewhere on the line between the two, at a random distance along it.
  4. Repeat until there are as many false alarms as correct rejections.

The new point is a blend of two real false alarms, so it sits in a part of the space where false alarms genuinely live — but it is not a copy of either. The effect is to fill in the rare class's territory rather than to pile up on its handful of known addresses.

SMOTE, balancing 14 false alarms against 211 rejections
SMOTE is applied to the training fold only — never the test fold. This matters more than it sounds. If you balanced the whole dataset first and then split it into folds, synthetic points built from a trial in fold 3 would end up in the training data for the model that is later tested on fold 3 — so the model would effectively have seen the answer, dressed up as a different trial. The AUC would come out flattering and meaningless. Balance inside each fold, after the split, and the test trials stay untouched.

And a limit worth keeping in mind: SMOTE adds no information. Every synthetic point is built from the same 14 real trials, and it can only fill the space between them. It stops the model taking the lazy option; it does not conjure evidence that was never recorded. If FA vs CR looks weak in chapter 6, this is part of why.
