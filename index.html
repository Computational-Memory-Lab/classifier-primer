Can we predict whether someone remembers a word from their brain activity?

A hands-on introduction to predicting memory from single trials of EEG: what the experiment is, what a classifier does, and what the results actually show. It assumes no background in machine learning and no programming. You will build the intuition by working with the real data rather than an illustration of it.

The short version

A participant studies a list of words. A few minutes later they see a mix of studied and unstudied words and call each one old or new. We record their EEG the whole time.

The traditional way to analyze this is to average hundreds of trials together and show that some bump in the waveform is reliably bigger for remembered words. That is the chart above, and it is real. But it only establishes that the activity is correlated with memory. It does not show the activity is doing anything.

A classifier sets a harder bar. We give a computer program one single trial's brain activity — not an average, one trial — and ask it to predict what happened. If it can predict trials it has never seen, we have narrowed in on activity that is actually memory-relevant.

How we score it: AUC

We need one number for “how well did it predict”. The obvious choice, the percentage of trials it got right, turns out to be badly misleading here, because the two things being told apart are rarely equally common. A classifier can score very well simply by always guessing whichever is more frequent, and never look at the EEG at all.

So the standard measure is AUC — the area under the ROC curve. Instead of forcing a yes/no answer, the classifier rates every trial by how confident it is, and AUC asks a question about the ordering of those ratings:

Take one random trial of each kind. How often does the classifier rate them in the right order? That proportion is the AUC. It ignores how common each kind is, so the always-guess-the-common-one trick scores exactly 0.5.

0.5 is chance — the ordering is no better than a coin flip. 1.0 is perfect — every trial of one kind is rated above every trial of the other. Chapters 2 and 4 build this up properly; the scale is all you need for now.

What we actually find
One AUC per participant, averaged over 67 participants.
Read that honestly. An AUC of 0.58 is a long way from 1.0. Single-trial EEG carries real memory information — reliably, across dozens of participants — but not very much of it. That modest number is the finding, not a bug, and the rest of this site is about what follows from it.

What this site covers

Eight short chapters. Chapters 1–4 assume nothing at all. Chapter 5 lets you train a real classifier in your browser, chapter 6 is the results, chapter 7 is the open research question, and chapter 8 is where to go next.

Where the numbers on this site come from

Everything quoted here is computed from the real data rather than typed in by hand. The group results are recomputed from the per-participant classifier outputs stored in the repository; the EEG traces and the trainable classifier in chapter 5 are derived from one participant's recording on the lab Drive.

The EEG was recorded by Chen et al. (2014): 67 participants, 256 electrodes sampled 250 times a second. Where the files came from and how they were checked is documented in the repository. This site ships only small derived summaries — no raw EEG, nothing identifiable.
