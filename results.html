Chapter 6

What we have found so far

The results. They are real, they are reliable, and they are small — and the smallness is the interesting part.

Every participant, one dot each

Chapter 5 showed one person's AUC bouncing around by hundredths. The answer is to do it for everyone and look at the whole distribution. Each dot below is one participant's cross-validated AUC — the number you produced yourself in the last chapter, computed 67 times.

All 67 participants, by task and classifier
Each dot is one person. The thick line is the group mean; the box is its 95% confidence interval. Hover any dot.

Two things jump out. The mean sits a little above the chance line and its confidence interval clears it comfortably — the effect is real. And a good number of individual participants land below chance. That is not a contradiction; it is what a small true effect plus noise looks like. Nobody's brain is anti-predictive. Their AUC just landed on the low side of a distribution centred slightly above 0.5.

The group is the result; the individual is not. This is why you will never see a claim about a single participant in this project, and why the statistics below are one-sample t-tests over participants, each contributing a single number.

LDA edges out SVM on every task. That is worth sitting with for a moment, because it is the opposite of what people expect: the simpler, older, faster method wins. With a few hundred trials and 280 features, a fancier model has nothing to work with — there is simply not enough data for extra flexibility to pay off. Chapter 4's overfitting curve is the reason.

Does the pipeline choice matter?

This is the actual research question, so here is the actual answer. Each row below is a full re-run of everything with one thing changed.

Preprocessing variants
Recorded runs from docs/multivariate_parameter_testing_results.txt. Click a task to compare.

The honest summary: it barely matters. Throwing out artifact-contaminated trials, applying a Laplacian spatial filter, excluding participants on behavioural grounds — all of it moves the AUC by a hundredth or two, in a range where the fold shuffle alone moves it by a hundredth. The Laplacian is perhaps a nose ahead, especially for FA vs CR.

A null result that is genuinely useful. If these choices do not matter much, future studies can stop agonising over them and fix them in advance — which is precisely what the manuscript sets out to provide.

The sweep that did matter

One thing changed the answer a lot. Instead of feeding the classifier the raw voltage, feed it a transformed version — the absolute value, the square, the cube.

This is not idle curiosity. Squaring is close to what computing power does, and power is the basis of the spectral features that chapter 7 is about. So this sweep is a direct test: is the spectral advantage just a nonlinear transformation in disguise?

Feature transformations
LDA, moving bins, from docs/feature_label_fixed_bins_results.txt. N = 61 (61 for Old/New and Hit/Miss, 57 for FA/CR).

Absolute value and squaring destroy the signal. FA vs CR falls to 0.497 and 0.494 — statistically indistinguishable from a coin flip (p = 0.83 and p = 0.69). Old/New drops from 0.579 to about 0.53. Cube, which preserves the sign, survives much better.

That pattern is a clue, and it points at the sign. Absolute value and squaring both throw away whether the voltage was positive or negative; cube keeps it. The information the classifier was using is substantially in the sign, and any transform that discards it discards the signal. So whatever makes spectral features good, it is not simply that they pass voltage through a nonlinearity — that operation on its own only makes things worse.

This is the manuscript's first real answer, and it is a negative one: it rules out the simplest explanation for the spectral advantage. In the abstract's words, “the spectrographic advantage was not attributable to voltage being passed through a nonlinear transformation.”

How many features is the right number?

The 280 features come from a 100 ms window sliding in 40 ms steps. Change those two numbers and you get a different feature count. Does it help?

Bin size and step size
LDA on raw features. Hover a point for the exact configuration.

Essentially flat from 120 features to 570, with a gentle peak around 190–280 and a slight decline at 570 — the overfitting you would predict from chapter 4. The pipeline's 280 is close enough to optimal, and nothing was left on the table by choosing it.

What about the classic ERP components?

For comparison, the manuscript also ran the traditional analysis: take one number per trial from the FN400 window and one from the LPP window, and see how well each predicts on its own.

The famous components, used one at a time, predict almost nothing — 0.508 is a rounding error away from chance, even though it is “statistically significant” across 67 people. Using all 280 features together roughly triples the distance above chance.

That is the entire argument of chapter 2 in one table. The FN400 is a real, replicable, decades-old effect. It is also nearly useless for predicting what any individual trial will do. Both are true, and only the classifier could tell you the second one.
