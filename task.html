Chapter 1

The memory task

Everything else on this site depends on understanding this one experiment. It is simple, and it is worth five minutes.

Study, distract, test — nine times over

Participants sit in front of a screen wearing a net of 256 electrodes. The experiment runs in three-part blocks, and the whole thing repeats nine times.

One block of the experiment
Repeated nine times, giving 225 studied words and 450 test words per participant.

The words are ordinary 4–8 letter nouns drawn from the Toronto Word Pool — chapter, artist, motor. The arithmetic in the middle is not measuring anything. It is there to occupy the participant so they cannot keep rehearsing the list, which removes recency effects: without it, the last few studied words would still be sitting in short-term memory at test and would be recognised from that rather than from having been learned. The distractor forces every word to be retrieved from memory proper.

Four things can happen on a test trial

At test, each word is either old (it was in the study list) or new (it was not). The participant presses one of two keys to say which they think it is. Cross those two facts and you get four outcomes — and these four labels are what the entire project is built on.

Three questions hide inside those four outcomes

Here is the step that takes people a while to see. Because every trial carries both what the word was and what the participant said, you can slice the same trials three different ways — and each slice asks a genuinely different psychological question.

The same 450 trials, sliced three ways
Click a task to see which trials it uses and which are set aside. Counts are participant 45's.
Why this matters. Old vs New asks whether the brain registers that a word was studied at all. Hit vs Miss asks whether the brain predicts success — same physical words, sorted by whether memory worked. FA vs CR asks about error — same physical words (all new), sorted by whether the person was fooled. Only the last two hold the stimulus constant and vary the memory, which is what makes them the interesting ones.

How good are people at this, really?

Before asking a computer to read the EEG, it is worth knowing how well the participants themselves did. Each dot is one of the 67 people in the dataset.

The measure is d′, and it exists because raw accuracy is easy to fake. Someone who answers “old” to everything gets every old word right, and a 100% hit rate looks excellent until you notice they also called every new word old. d′ subtracts that away: it is the hit rate minus the false-alarm rate, both converted to standard-deviation units, so it only rewards genuinely separating the two. Zero means the rates are identical — no ability to tell old from new. This is the same logic AUC uses for the classifier, applied to the person.

Behaviour of all 67 participants
Hover any dot. d′ (“d-prime”) is the standard measure of how well someone tells old words from new ones. It is the distance between two rates, expressed in standard deviations: how often they called an old word “old” (the hit rate) versus how often they called a new word “old” (the false-alarm rate).

People are clearly not at chance — they can do this task. But they are also far from perfect, and they vary a lot. Hold on to that spread: when we get to classifier results in chapter 6, this same participant-to-participant variability shows up again, and it is one of the reasons the lab needs dozens of people rather than one very well-measured person.
