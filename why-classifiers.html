Chapter 2

Why classifiers, not just averages

This chapter is the argument for the whole project. If you take one idea away from this site, take this one.

A single trial looks like nothing at all

Here is the honest starting point. This is one real second of brain activity from one real trial — participant 45, one electrode, one word they went on to remember. Look at it and try to see the memory.

Watch an average appear out of noise
Drag the slider to average more Hit trials together. These are the real recorded trials, added in one at a time.

At one trial it is a mess. By ten you can see something. By a hundred a clean, smooth, textbook waveform has appeared — the sharp negative dip around 100 ms, the slow drift afterwards. Nothing was added. The signal was in there the whole time; averaging just cancelled the noise around it.

This is what ERP research is. Averaging is not a trick — it is a genuinely powerful tool, and essentially everything psychology knows about the electrical signature of memory came from it. The problem is not that it is wrong. The problem is what it throws away.

What the average costs you

Now put the two conditions side by side. Averaging within each participant, and then across all 67 of them, gives the classic result: words that were remembered produce a slightly different waveform from new words correctly called new.

The classic old/new effect, across 67 participants

There it is — decades of memory research in one picture. Note the size of it: a fraction of a microvolt. And note what it took to see: 67 people × 450 trials each, roughly 30,000 trials averaged down into two lines.

Now here is the same comparison for one participant, with the actual trials that went into it drawn underneath.

One participant, and the trials underneath their average
Participant 45. Toggle the faint single trials on — the bold lines are the averages of exactly those trials.

That picture is the whole difficulty in one image. The difference between the averages is real — it replicates across labs and decades. But an average is a statement about a group of trials. It cannot tell you anything about any particular trial, because the moment you averaged, the individual trials stopped existing.

So the classic finding says: studied words produce, on average, a slightly different waveform. It does not say: this brain activity is what remembering is made of. Those are very different claims, and only the first one has been demonstrated.

Description is easy. Prediction is hard.

There is a deeper problem, and it is the reason machine learning people are fussy in ways that can seem pedantic at first.

If you go looking for a difference in a rich enough dataset, you will find one. 257 electrodes × 325 time points is 83,525 places to look. Some of them will differ between your conditions by chance. If you then report the ones that differed, you are describing the noise in the sample you happened to collect — and it will not be there next time.

Finding a “result” in pure noise
Both groups below are random numbers with no real difference whatsoever. Search enough electrodes and time points, and a “significant” effect appears anyway.

Nothing in that demo is real. There is no signal to find. Yet with enough places to look, something always crosses the line — and it is easy to write it up as a finding, complete with a p-value.

The fix is not more statistics. It is a different question. Instead of asking “is there a difference in the data I already have?”, ask “can I predict data I have never seen?” You cannot fool yourself the same way, because the answer is checked against trials that had no say in building the model.

So: what is a classifier?

A classifier is a program that learns a rule from labelled examples. You show it many trials, each one paired with its answer — this trial was a Hit, that one was a Miss — and it works out a rule for telling them apart. Then you show it trials it has never seen and make it guess.

The crucial discipline is that the trials it is tested on are held back from training entirely. If the model has seen a trial, that trial cannot be used to judge it. The test has to be fair, or it tells you nothing.

This is exactly the argument Chakravarty, Chen & Caplan (2020) made for the study phase of this very dataset, and it is the paper this project follows. In their words, the field distinguishes “descriptive analysis, subject to overfitting, and true prediction, that can classify untrained data.”

How we score it: AUC

We need one number for “how well did it predict”. Accuracy — the percentage it got right — turns out to be a bad choice here. Remember participant 45's 14 false alarms against 211 correct rejections? A classifier that ignores the brain entirely and always says “correct rejection” scores 94% accuracy. It is useless and it looks excellent.

So we use AUC instead. Rather than forcing a yes/no decision, the classifier rates each trial by how confident it is. AUC then asks a question that does not care about class imbalance:

Pick one random Hit and one random Miss. What is the probability the classifier rated the Hit higher? That probability is the AUC. 0.5 means it is guessing. 1.0 means it never gets the ordering wrong.

Because it only depends on the ordering of trials, an always-say-CR classifier scores exactly 0.5 — correctly identified as worthless. Chapter 4 builds this up visually. For now, the scale is all you need:
