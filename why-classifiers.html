Chapter 2

Why classifiers, not just averages

This chapter is the argument for the whole project: why a classifier is worth the trouble when averaging is so much simpler.

A single trial looks like nothing at all

Here is the honest starting point. This is 1.3 seconds of real EEG from one real trial — participant 45, one electrode, one word they went on to remember. Look at it: there is nothing you can point to and call a memory. That is not a shortcoming of the chart — it is the problem the whole field starts from.

Watch an average appear out of noise
Drag the slider to average more Hit trials together. These are the real recorded trials, added in one at a time — every trial in the average is drawn faintly behind it.

At one trial it is a mess. By ten you can see something. By a hundred a clean, smooth, textbook waveform has appeared — the sharp negative dip around 100 ms, the slow drift afterwards. Nothing was added. The signal was in there the whole time; averaging just cancelled the noise around it.

This is what ERP research is. An ERP is an event-related potential: the average voltage across many trials of the same kind, lined up on the moment the event happened — here, the word appearing. The chart above is one. Averaging is a standard and effective tool, and most of what is known about the electrical correlates of memory was established with it. The issue is not accuracy. It is that an average discards the individual trials, and those are what the rest of this site needs.

What the average costs you

Now put the two conditions side by side. Averaging within each participant, and then across all 67 of them, gives the classic result: words that were remembered produce a slightly different waveform from new words correctly called new.

The classic old/new effect, across 67 participants

That is the standard result. Two things about it are worth noting: the difference is a fraction of a microvolt, and seeing it took 67 people contributing 450 trials each — roughly 30,000 trials reduced to two lines.

Now here is the same comparison for one participant, with the actual trials that went into it drawn underneath.

One participant, and the trials underneath their average
Participant 45. Toggle the faint single trials on — the bold lines are the averages of exactly those trials.

That picture is the whole difficulty in one image. The difference between the averages is real — it replicates across labs and decades. But an average is a statement about a group of trials. It cannot tell you anything about any particular trial, because the moment you averaged, the individual trials stopped existing.

So the classic finding says: studied words produce, on average, a slightly different waveform. It does not say that a single trial, from a single person, reflects remembering. Those are very different claims, and only the first one has been demonstrated.

Description is easy. Prediction is hard.

There is a deeper problem, and it is the reason machine learning people are fussy in ways that can seem pedantic at first.

If you go looking for a difference in a rich enough dataset, you will find one. 256 electrodes × 325 time points is 83,200 places to look. Some of them will differ between your conditions by chance. If you then report the ones that differed, you are describing the noise in the sample you happened to collect, and it will not be there next time.

Finding a “result” in pure noise
Both groups below are random numbers with no real difference whatsoever. Search enough electrodes and time points, and a “significant” effect appears anyway.

Nothing in that demo is real. There is no signal to find. Yet with enough places to look, something always crosses the line — and it is easy to write it up as a finding, complete with a p-value.

The fix is not more statistics. It is a different question. Instead of asking “is there a difference in the data I already have?”, ask “can I predict data I have never seen?” You cannot fool yourself the same way, because the answer is checked against trials that had no say in building the model.

So: what is a classifier?

A classifier is a program that learns from labelled examples. You show it many trials, each paired with its answer — this one was a Hit, that one was a Miss — and it finds a weighting of the 280 features that best separates the two groups. Because it weighs many measurements at once rather than one at a time, this is called a multivariate approach — as opposed to the univariate ERP analysis, which takes a single number per trial. Concretely, it assigns each feature a number saying how much that feature counts and in which direction, then scores a new trial by multiplying its 280 values by those weights and adding them up. A high score means Hit, a low one means Miss. Chapter 4 shows this happening on screen.

The crucial discipline is that the trials it is tested on are held back from training entirely. If the model has already seen a trial, it can get that trial right by having memorized its quirks rather than by having learned anything general — and memorizing is exactly what a model with 280 knobs and a few hundred examples is good at. Scoring it on trials it trained on measures how well it memorized, and tells you nothing about whether it would work on a new participant, a new session, or tomorrow. The test has to be on unseen trials or it answers the wrong question.

This is exactly the argument Chakravarty, Chen & Caplan (2020) made for the study phase of this very dataset, and it is the paper this project follows. In their words, the field distinguishes “descriptive analysis, subject to overfitting, and true prediction, that can classify untrained data.”

How we score it: AUC

We need one number for “how well did it predict”. Accuracy — the percentage it got right — turns out to be a bad choice here. Remember participant 45's 14 false alarms against 211 correct rejections? A classifier that ignores the EEG entirely and always answers “correct rejection” gets every one of those 211 right and all 14 false alarms wrong:

211 / (211 + 14) = 0.938 — 93.8% accurate, and completely useless

So we use AUC instead — short for area under the curve, meaning the area under the ROC curve that chapter 4 builds. Rather than forcing a yes/no decision, the classifier rates each trial by how confident it is that it is a false alarm. AUC then asks a question that does not care how common each class is:

Pick one random false alarm and one random correct rejection. What is the probability the classifier rated the false alarm higher? That probability is the AUC.

You can compute it by brute force: take every possible pairing of one false alarm with one correct rejection, count how many the classifier ordered correctly, and divide.

AUC = (pairs ordered correctly + ½ × ties) / (false alarms × correct rejections)

For participant 45 that is 14 × 211 = 2,954 pairs. Get all of them right and AUC = 2,954/2,954 = 1.0. Order them at random and you win about half, so AUC ≈ 0.5. Get every pair backwards and you score 0 — which would be a perfect classifier with its labels swapped.

Notice what the always-say-“correct rejection” classifier does here. It gives every trial the same score, so every one of those 2,954 pairs is a tie, the ½ applies to all of them, and the AUC is exactly 0.5 — correctly identified as worthless, where accuracy called it 93.8%. Because the count of pairs sits in the denominator, it makes no difference that one class is fifteen times rarer than the other. That immunity is the whole reason we use it.
